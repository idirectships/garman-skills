How do we classify the severity of this incident?
Single choice

Incident severity determines the scope of the post-mortem process, who needs to be involved, and what level of remediation is required. Over-classifying wastes engineering time on bureaucratic process. Under-classifying misses systemic risk.

Severity is about business impact and blast radius — not just technical complexity. A 30-second API blip that hit 0 users is lower severity than a 2-minute degradation that silently corrupted 200 records.

2
Who owns writing the post-mortem document?
Single choice

Post-mortem authorship determines whose mental model of the incident gets preserved. The on-call engineer has the most operational detail but often the least time. A designated writer role produces cleaner docs but risks losing important context through the retelling gap.

The author is NOT the person who caused the incident. Post-mortems are blameless — the author is the incident coordinator or lead responder.

3
Which remediation categories should we track action items under?
Multi-select

Action items without categories drift into a backlog and never ship. Categorizing remediation items helps route them to the right owners and prioritize them against other work. Not all categories apply to every incident — select what fits this specific case.

Common pitfall: marking everything as "monitoring improvement" when the real issue was deployment process or capacity planning.

4
What is the target timeline for completing all action items?
Single choice

Without a deadline, post-mortem action items sit in JIRA forever. Too-short deadlines create false urgency and cause half-baked fixes. A 2-4 week window balances speed with thoroughness for most SEV-2 incidents.

The timeline should be set BEFORE the meeting ends — not retroactively applied. Items not completed by the deadline get escalated automatically.

5
Should we send a public status page update?
Single choice

Public communication builds trust if done proactively and accurately. Sending a status update after a user-reported incident is reactive and appears defensive. Not sending one for a visible outage looks like a cover-up.

The bar: would a customer reading our status page be confused by a gap in the incident timeline? If yes, post an update.

6
What does the 5-why analysis identify as root cause tier?
Single choice

The 5-why analysis is meant to surface the SYSTEMIC root cause, not stop at the proximate cause. If every post-mortem concludes with "engineer made a mistake," the 5-why analysis is not going deep enough.

Root cause tiers: Tier 1 = code bug (proximate). Tier 2 = missing test/review gate. Tier 3 = process gap. Tier 4 = organizational or incentive misalignment. Most actionable post-mortems reach Tier 2 or 3.
